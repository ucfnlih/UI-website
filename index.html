Research Stories

Example Narratives

How this data - and its predecessor, state-level unemployment risk - has been used to answer real labor market questions.

Built on State-Level Unemployment Risk

Case 01
arXiv:2601.02554

Public debate links worsening job prospects for AI-exposed occupations to the release of ChatGPT in late 2022. Using monthly U.S. unemployment insurance records, we measure occupation- and location-specific unemployment risk and find that risk rose in AI-exposed occupations beginning in early 2022 — months before ChatGPT's release. Analyzing millions of LinkedIn profiles, we show that graduate cohorts from 2021 onward entered AI-exposed jobs at lower rates than earlier cohorts, with gaps opening before late 2022. From millions of university syllabi, we also find that graduates taking more AI-exposed curricula had higher first-job pay and shorter job searches after ChatGPT — suggesting the technology reshapes, rather than simply destroys, labor market opportunities.

Case 02
PNAS Nexus 2025

Standard employment statistics — such as total state unemployment rates — are inadequate proxies for understanding which workers face the greatest risk. In March 2020, during the rise of the 2020 US COVID Recession, states with the highest total unemployment rates were not the same states where Healthcare Support occupations experienced the highest unemployment risk (right panel). This divergence reveals that aggregate statistics mask critical variation across occupations and geographies that our occupation-level measure can capture.

Case 03
PNAS Nexus 2025

A key finding of our work is that an occupation's employment share is not a reliable proxy for its unemployment risk. Many occupation–state pairs deviate from aggregate trends in ways that are invisible to standard labor market statistics. For example, unemployment risk for Computer and Math occupations in Nebraska is positively correlated with employment share over time, while Management occupations in New Hampshire show no such correlation. These heterogeneous patterns motivate fine-grained, occupation-level measures of labor market distress — the foundation this county-level dataset extends.

Methodology

How We Built This Dataset

Unemployment risk is estimated at the county x industry x month level using observed claims and a validated imputation model.

[Risk]

Unemployment Risk

Rather than raw claim counts, we compute unemployment risk as a normalized measure of labor market stress within each county-industry cell, enabling comparisons across counties of different sizes.

Unemployment Risk =
UI Claims / (UI Claims + Employment)
[Sources]

Data Sources

Observed UI claims come from state workforce agencies - the exact set of states differs between initial and continued claims. Employment denominators are from BLS QCEW (quarterly, aggregated to monthly). Demographic covariates are from Census ACS 5-year estimates (DP03, 2015-2023).

[Model]

Imputation Model

For states without observed claims, a two-stage hurdle model predicts values: a logistic classifier predicts claim presence; a count regression estimates magnitude. Validated via Leave-One-State-Out (LOSO) cross-validation, run separately for initial and continued claims.

[Confidence]

Confidence Scores

Confidence is estimated entirely from predicted outputs - never from actual counts - so scores generalize to unseen states. Five signals are combined via a Ridge-regression-learned weighting:

location quotient . prediction ratio
p_positive . zero rate . entropy
[Flags]

Missing Value Flags

Two distinct flags distinguish states where data does not exist from states that explicitly declined to share data, allowing downstream users to filter by data provenance.

[NAICS]

NAICS Sector Grouping

Industries are reported at 2-digit NAICS level. Sectors 32/33 -> 31 (Manufacturing); 45 -> 44 (Retail); 49 -> 48 (Transportation) to ensure consistent cell coverage across states and time.
