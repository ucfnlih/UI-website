Methodology

How We Built This Dataset

Unemployment risk is estimated at the county × industry × month level. For states that do not report claims data, a hurdle model predicts values, with uncertainty quantified by a confidence score.

📊

Unemployment Risk

Rather than raw claim counts, we compute unemployment risk as a normalized measure of labor market stress within each county-industry cell. This makes values comparable across counties of different sizes.

Unemployment Risk =
UI Claims / (UI Claims + Employment)
🗺️

Data Sources

Observed UI claims come from 28 state workforce agencies. Employment denominators are sourced from the BLS Quarterly Census of Employment and Wages (QCEW), aggregated to monthly counts. Demographic covariates come from Census ACS 5-year estimates (DP03 profile variables, 2015–2023).

🤖

Imputation Model

For the 22 states without observed claims, we use a two-stage hurdle model: a logistic classifier predicts whether a county-sector cell has any claims in a given month; a count regression then estimates the magnitude. The model is validated via Leave-One-State-Out (LOSO) cross-validation.

🎯

Confidence Scores

Because imputed states have no ground truth, confidence is estimated entirely from predicted outputs — never from actual counts. Four signals are combined:

Score = 0.5 × ratio
       + 0.3 × p_positive
       + 0.1 × zero_rate
       + 0.1 × entropy
✅

Missing Value Flags

The dataset distinguishes between two types of missing values: states for which data simply does not exist, and states that explicitly declined to share their data. Each is given a distinct flag so downstream users can filter appropriately.

📎

NAICS Sector Grouping

Industries are reported at the 2-digit NAICS level. Sectors 32/33 are merged into 31 (Manufacturing); 45 into 44 (Retail Trade); 49 into 48 (Transportation) to ensure consistent cell coverage across states and time periods.
